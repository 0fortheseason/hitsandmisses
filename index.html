Mock drafts are an exercise in optimism. Every analyst slots each player into their ideal situation and imagines the best-case outcome — a perfect fit for every hole on every roster. Reality is more sobering. Most prospects, even highly-touted ones, turn into a whole bunch of nothing.

This simulator is a cold-water antidote to mock draft mania. Rather than assuming every pick works out, it runs a weighted probability simulation for each player based on historical NFL draft hit rates by position and draft slot — derived from draft classes between 2000 and 2020, giving each player enough career time to fairly assess. A "hit" means the player became at least a reliable NFL starter.

The simulator doesn't decide outcomes — the historical rates do. The point isn't to predict the future; it's to reframe the conversation. Instead of asking "where does this player fit best?", ask: "given what we know about how often players at this position and slot actually pan out, what's a realistic set of outcomes for this class?"

The concept originated in a March 2025 post on 0 for the Season, where coin flips were used to assign hits and misses to a community mock draft — then each outcome was examined through the lens of coaching, opportunity, and player traits. This tool automates and scales that process.

This is speculative fiction about a projection of the NFL Draft. Don't get too worked up about it.
